Description

Data-driven AI model to identify prospective areas for mineral exploration

I developed a data-driven artificial intelligence model capable of predicting the concentration of minerals on the Earth's surface at a given geographical location. I use a database that compiles the information provided by mining companies during the last years of exploration. The data comprises tabulated information about the collected bedrock samples containing sample coordinates, chemical analysis results, and lithology. I also have a similar dataset consisting of sediment samples and a high-resolution magnetic field map of Québec.

I use a transfer learning technique to extract features from the Magnetic Field, sediment samples, and Lithology codes through a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), a Graph Neural Network (GNN), and an Artificial Neural Network (ANN), respectively, by keeping the last layer of each NN. The CNN will transform the magnetic field image surrounding every sample into a vector of features. Similarly, the GNN takes advantage of the spatial distribution of the samples to create a graph and uses graph convolutions and some connected layers to transform graphs into vectors of features—other features, consisting of the sample's distance to some. Finally, the ANN will transform large binary vectors obtained from string lithology codes into smaller float feature vectors. All features were used together to train a local ANN encoder-decoder (ANN-ED) and a local XGBoost encoder-decoder (XGB-ED).

The model's robustness is ensured through the most relevant and creative preprocessing tasks, including the meticulous imputation of missing values and data cleaning (denoising). For missing values imputation, I use XGB-ED. Then, I denoise the data through an iterative process: I train the ANN-ED model, clean, and retrain. This rigorous process results in a less noisy dataset and a more effective encoder-decoder, guaranteeing the model's accuracy in the later modeling phase.

The novel model, which consists of a weighted aggregation grid model for step-forward prediction followed by a fixed-point iteration correction using the ANN-ED, has demonstrated its predictive power. The results unequivocally show that the built features are highly predictive. Fig 1 presents a concentration heatmap for a particular mineral, with a resolution of 50x50 meters per pixel. The yellow balls represent the rock samples used to train the ANN-ED, and the five colors represent five different concentration ranges expressed in parts per billion.
